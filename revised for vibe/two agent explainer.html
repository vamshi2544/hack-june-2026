What problem are we solving?

A team ships a release. The health checks go green. But something downstream quietly broke — and nobody notices for hours. Then it’s a war room: digging through logs, asking “did we change anything?”, trying to remember if we’ve seen this before. The release was risky in ways no one flagged up front, and the failure was slow to explain.

The idea in one line: two AI teammates for the release lifecycle — one checks the risk before you ship, one watches after you ship and explains failures in minutes instead of hours.

ReleasePilot

before deploy · predict

When you’re about to ship, it gathers the history and the change, scores how risky this release is, and drafts the release page — so the team approves with eyes open, not blind.

ReleaseWatch

after deploy · detect & explain

After you ship, it watches the right signals, catches the drift that health checks miss, and when something breaks it assembles the evidence into a root-cause analysis — with a confidence score — and raises the incident.

Why two agents and not one?

Different jobs (predict vs. detect), different moments (before vs. after deploy), and — the key insight — a clean handoff between them. ReleasePilot doesn’t just disappear at deploy; it hands ReleaseWatch a risk packet: “here’s what I expect, here’s what to watch, here are the thresholds.” That’s why ReleaseWatch can tell a real problem from normal noise — it was briefed.

The end-to-end workflow

What actually happens, start to finish — the same story whether it runs on a platform or in our demo. Click any step to expand it, or press play to walk through. Three points need a human (the purple person icons).

highlights each step in order
Pre-release · ReleasePilot
Developer flags a release PILOT
When the work is ready to ship, the developer drops a comment on the release story (e.g. “@release-pilot, this is ready”). That’s the trigger — no new tool to learn.
Gathers the context PILOT
Pulls the change details, the services touched, past incidents on those services, and the standard release runbook. It’s reading the same places a senior engineer would — just instantly.
Scores the risk PILOT
Weighs the change and the history into a 0–100 risk score with plain reasons — e.g. 72 / Elevated because it touches messaging, needs a database migration, and matches a past incident. The score is computed, not guessed.
Drafts the release page PILOT
Fills the standard release runbook: the risk and why, the code diff, the checklist, links to past incidents. A blank page becomes a complete, reviewable release record.
Human reviews & approves GATE 1
A person reads the curated page and approves (or sends it back). The agent did the legwork; the human makes the call.
Human approves the deploy GATE 2
The change ticket advances toward Implement; a person approves the actual go. Then the release deploys.
🤝
The handoff (A2A). ReleasePilot passes ReleaseWatch a risk packet — what it expects, what to watch, and the thresholds. This is why the watcher knows a real problem from normal noise.
Post-release · ReleaseWatch
Watches quietly — all clear (L1) WATCH
Hourly checks. Health looks green. Everything within the thresholds the risk packet set. Nothing to say yet.
Spots unexplained drift (L2 · amber) WATCH
A metric moves in a way the risk packet did not predict. The agent flags it, posts to the team channel, and tightens its watch cadence. This is the catch that simple health checks miss.
Escalates & explains (L3 · red) WATCH
It assembles the evidence — logs, metrics, the code diff, matching past incidents — into a root-cause analysis with a confidence score (e.g. 0.84), and opens an incident. Minutes, not a multi-hour war room.
Human acknowledges GATE 3
On-call confirms the finding and applies the fix. The incident stays accountable to a person.
Recovery WATCH
The fix lands, metrics return to baseline, the watch relaxes. Loop closed.
The example we demo: a routing change needs a small database migration. It ran in the test environment (so tests passed) but not in the deploy environment. After deploy, health checks are green — but the publisher can’t start against the missing table and silently stops publishing. Three downstream consumers go to zero at the exact deploy minute. ReleaseWatch ties the silence to the deploy and a matching past incident at 0.84 confidence — the kind of thing that normally eats an afternoon.

Glossary — every term, in plain language

For anyone new to the concept. No prior knowledge assumed.

Agent
A program that can take a goal, decide the steps, use tools, and act — not just answer a question. Here: ReleasePilot and ReleaseWatch.
A2A (agent-to-agent) handoff
One agent passing work and context to another. ReleasePilot hands ReleaseWatch the risk packet at deploy.
Risk packet
The briefing ReleasePilot creates: the risk score, what it expects, what to watch, and the thresholds. It’s what makes ReleaseWatch smart instead of noisy.
MCP Gateway
A governed doorway for an agent to read from and write to real systems (Jira, Splunk, etc.) through typed, sanctioned tools — instead of ad-hoc API keys.
LLM Gateway
A governed doorway to an approved large language model. It’s where the agents do their reasoning — the risk narrative and the root-cause analysis.
HITL (human-in-the-loop)
Points where a person must approve before the agent proceeds. We have three: review, deploy, acknowledge.
Drift
When a live metric moves away from what was expected. Unexplained drift — not predicted by the risk packet — is the warning sign.
RCA (root-cause analysis)
The explanation of why something broke, backed by evidence (logs, metrics, the diff, past incidents) and a confidence score.
L1 / L2 / L3
Escalation levels. L1 = hourly all-clear. L2 = amber, unexplained drift, faster cadence + alert. L3 = red, confirmed problem, RCA + incident.
Vibe coding
Building software by directing an AI coding assistant (here, GitHub Copilot) conversationally — the theme of the hackathon.
Seam
A marked spot in the code where the demo fakes something (data or reasoning) that production does for real — written so it’s a one-line swap.
Agentic AI Platform
The enterprise platform that hosts agents, governs their reasoning (LLM Gateway) and their actions (MCP Gateway), stores their memory, and enforces human approvals.
