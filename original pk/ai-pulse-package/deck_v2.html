The problem

Every release fails in the same window —
the first 24 hours after deploy.

Not because engineers are careless. A deploy succeeds, the app reports healthy — and a downstream link silently dies. Nothing reads the relationship between systems. At 2 AM on a Sunday, that gap is where response time goes to die.

The idea

Two agents. One lifecycle.
A handoff that carries context.

ReleasePilot · pre-deploy
Reads the Jira story, pulls past incidents, resolves downstream apps, and assembles a risk packet — what's risky about this release and what to watch for.
ReleaseWatch · post-deploy
Reads the risk packet, watches the deploy, escalates proportionally, and generates an evidence-backed root cause when something drifts.
The risk packet
passed Pilot → Watch via A2A, persisted in AgentCore Memory. It's what makes post-deploy monitoring contextual instead of generic.

Why this works

Three principles.

01
Two agents share an artifact
The risk packet handoff via A2A means state lives in the artifact, not the infrastructure. Recovery is automatic; the handoff is the insight.
02
Humans approve the big decisions
Three HITL gates. The agent does the homework so humans decide in seconds. It never advances a major decision alone.
03
Cross-system over per-system
Reading metrics across systems against release intent catches relationship failures that per-system thresholds miss entirely.

Defending the pitch · internal

Anticipated questions.

Is this real or a mockup?
A deliberate TypeScript prototype of the agent experience. Production is Python on AgentCore with Claude via the LLM Gateway. We have a slide showing exactly what's real vs simulated. The L3 prompt is real and tested.
How is this different from existing AIOps?
AIOps catches anomalies per system. Here every system looked fine — the app was green, the consumers just stopped receiving. We catch the relationship by reading release intent. The risk packet — knowing what the deploy intended — is what no AIOps tool has.
Why two agents, why A2A?
A2A is a platform primitive. Clean ownership boundaries. The risk-packet handoff is what makes post-deploy monitoring contextual. ReleaseWatch can also run standalone for manual deploys.
What if the RCA is wrong?
Confidence is calibrated and capped at 0.95 — never certainty. The agent never takes the major action; humans approve. The L3 card always offers Downgrade. AgentCore Evaluations flags misses for tuning.
What we'd need to build this for real · internal

From prototype to production.

Platform access
AgentCore Runtime + the LLM Gateway (Claude) + MCP Gateway connectors for Jira, ServiceNow, Confluence, Splunk, New Relic — most already in the catalog.
Event wiring
EventBridge/Kafka hookup to Jira webhooks, CHG state changes, and UDeploy deploy-completion events.
Risk packet calibration
Per-application-family drift thresholds and expected-shift templates — the hardest and highest-value tuning, via AgentCore Evaluations.
A scoped pilot
One application family, human-in-the-loop on every escalation, measuring RCA accuracy and time-to-recovery against the baseline, then expand.
The agent logic is modest — hundreds of lines of Python plus four tested prompts. The work is integration. With platform access, this is weeks, not months.
